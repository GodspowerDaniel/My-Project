The course then taught us how to deep dive into a book, make annotations, how to challenge the author, its historicism, and your very own thoughts. The process can be distilled to three rules:

1. Reading is never an isolated act.
2. Effective reading is quality over quantity.
3. It’s not about who you are at the beginning of the book. It’s who you become at the end of it.

To most people, reading is a casual act: scrolling down paragraphs, flipping one page to another, filling up spare time. There are among us those who are quantitative about reading — the number of books you’ve read is the barometer for your mental acuity.

The course I was forced to take took a qualitative approach. You could read just one book in a whole year, but that book becomes a journey of self-discovery, a literary voyage that enriches your cognitive library.

The book you read will accompany you for life. That book can be read again and again, each time using the same approach, and you will rediscover pockets of imagination you never knew existed. It was Oscar Wilde who said, “If one cannot enjoy reading a book over and over again, there is no use in reading it at all.”

Being a purposeful reader begins by asking questions as you read. A clean book is one that has not been read. And yes, it can be exhausting. That is an indicator that you’re reading correctly, as pointed out by William Styron in ‘Conversations with William Styron’: “A great book should leave you with many experiences, and slightly exhausted at the end. You live several lives while reading.”

What that seems to suggest is that casual reading is a good pastime but without being sedulous, what you read becomes fleeting.

Now, you’re probably thinking aloud, “No wonder I don’t recall anything from that book I read at the airport lounge…”

“Books are the quietest and most constant of friends; they are the most accessible and wisest of counselors, and the most patient of teachers.” ― Charles W. Eliot, ‘The Durable Satisfactions of Life’



When reading, you’re not alone. You’re never alone. There is the author walking along the path with you. Ask the author questions. Challenge the author as if he/she is right there seated opposite you. Begin with what you agree or disagree with the author. “This is ridiculous Dickens! I don’t agree that Bill Sikes is more malicious than Fagin who is the bigger villain because he is essentially a groomer…” Did the author’s actions challenge your principles? Did it bother you? Why? Did it frustrate you? If you could rewrite the situation, what would you do differently? All of these are written on the side of the page. Yes, margins on a page have a purpose — to be scribbled on. You are to make a mess of the entire book with your annotations. Remember what Stephen King said, “If you don’t have time to read, you don’t have the time (or the tools) to write. Simple as that.” If you’re one to feel fearful of ‘soiling’ a book with ideas, lead and ink, your reading depth may also come into question. Although you can write separately in a journal, having the thoughts written on the same page, in between the lines, surrounding circled keywords, helps to put you, your thinking and the author on the same page, literally and metaphorically.

By the time you reached the end of your book, it has been a profound and enlightening experience. With a collection of such endeavors, it rings differently when I ask the following question: If you could only keep one book in your library that signifies your life, what book would that be?
